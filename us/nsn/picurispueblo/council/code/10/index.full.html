Title 10 Children

Chapter 10.01 General Provisions

10.01.010 Title.

This title shall be known and may be cited as the "Pueblo of Picuris Children's Code" and references in this title to "Code" shall refer to this Code unless another is clearly indicated.

Prior Codifications

§10.01.010 was formerly codified as 10-1-1

10.01.020 Purpose, Meaning and Severability.

(a) It is the purpose of this Children's Code to:

(1) Recognize that the children of the Pueblo of Picuris are its most important resource and their welfare is of paramount importance;

(2) Safeguard, protect, and secure for each child coming under the scope of this Code, the care and guidance, preferably in the child's own home or community, as will serve the emotional, mental and physical welfare of the child and the best interest of the Tribe;

(3) Preserve and strengthen family ties whenever possible; to preserve and strengthen the child's cultural and ethnic identity;

(4) Secure for any child(ren) removed from his/her home the care, guidance and control to help him/her develop into responsible, well-adjusted adults; and

(5) Protect the peace and security of the community and its individual residents.

(b) The Pueblo of Picuris Children's Code shall be liberally interpreted and construed to fulfill the purpose of this Code.

(c) If any Section of the Pueblo of Picuris Children's Code is found to be invalid, the remainder of this Code shall not be affected thereby.

10.01.030 Definitions.

"Abandon" means when a parent or extended family leaves a child without adequate communication or fails to support a child and there is no indication of the parent(s) or extended family's willingness to assume the parental role(s) consistently for a period of no less than six (6) months.

"Abuse" includes physical, emotional, psychological and sexual abuse and are defined as follows.

a. "Physical abuse" includes placing a minor knowingly, intentionally, or negligently in a situation that may endanger his/her life or health; knowingly or intentionally torturing, beating, cruelly confining or punishing a minor;

b. "Emotional abuse" includes systematic patterns of belittling, scapegoating, or other non-physical forms of openly hostile or rejecting actions, as well as excessive non- physical discipline and verbal threats or other forms of maltreatment such as abandonment, beating and sexual assault;

c. "Sexual abuse" means criminal sexual penetration, incest, criminal sexual contact and non-contact sexual exploitation which are defined as follows:

1. "Criminal sexual contact of a minor" is when a person unlawfully and intentionally touches or applies force to the intimate parts of a minor who is under eighteen (18) years of age, or causes that minor to touch his/her intimate parts;

2. "Criminal sexual penetration of a minor" is when a person unlawfully and intentionally causes a minor who is under eighteen (18) years of age, to engage in sexual intercourse, cunnilingus, fellatio or anal intercourse or causes penetration, to any extent and with any object, of the genital or anal openings of another whether or not there is any emission, through the use of coercion that results in personal injury to the victim;

3. "Incest" is when a person knowingly marries or has sexual intercourse with a person within the following degrees of blood relationship: parents and children, including grandparents and grandchildren of every degree; brothers and sisters of the half, as well as of the whole blood; uncles and nieces/nephews; aunts and nephews/nieces; first degree cousins;

4. "Non-contact sexual exploitation" is committed when an adult, for their sexual gratification: exposes intimate parts of their body, including the genitals, anus or breasts, to a minor; forces a minor to watch sexual acts or sexual violence; forces the minor to perform sexual acts while being watched by the adult; forces the minor to listen to threats and/or sexual details; or, watches a minor undress.

"Adult" is a person eighteen (18) years of age or older who is subject to the jurisdiction of the Pueblo of Picuris.

"Child" is a person under fourteen (14) years of age.

"Custodian" is a person other than a parent or guardian who is providing food, shelter, and supervision to a child.

"Contempt of Court" is the willful disobedience or interference of the Court.

"Delinquent Act" is an act committed by a juvenile which is designated a crime under the Pueblo of Picuris Criminal Code.

"Detention Facility" is a facility in which a minor may be placed for a period of temporary custody prior to disposition by the Pueblo of Picuris Juvenile Court.

"Domicile" means the permanent legal residence of an individual. The domicile of a child is presumed to be that of the child's mother unless proven otherwise.

"Extended Family" is a person (tribal member) who has reached the age of eighteen (18), who is the minor's grandparent, aunt or uncle, brother or sister, brother-in-law or sister-in- law, niece or nephew, first or second cousin, stepparent, godparent or traditionally adopted individual.

"Foster Care" is a child welfare service, which provides substitute family care for a child for a period when his/her own family cannot care for them. The goal of foster care or any placement is a permanent family for the child. It includes all out of home care when it is officially sanctioned by Bureau of Indian Affairs Northern Pueblos Agency social services or human services. Out of home care covers, voluntary and involuntary placements. They may be short or long-term, temporary or emergency placement, with a family, group home, or institution.

"Guardian" is a person other than the minor's parent who is by law responsible for the care and custody of that minor or his/her estate or both.

"Guardian Ad Litem" is an adult appointed by the Court who is to represent a child in any suit to which he/she may be a party for the protection of the child's interest.

"Indian" is any member of a federally recognized tribe or a person eligible for membership in a federally recognized tribe.

"Indian Custodian" is an adult Indian to whom temporary physical care, custody and control has been transferred by the parent of such minor.

"Juvenile Offender" is a person who commits a delinquent act prior to their eighteenth (18th) birthday.

"Legal Custody" is a legal status created by a Court that vests in a person the right and duty to protect the custody of a minor, the right and duty to protect, train and discipline the minor and to provide him/her with food, shelter, education and ordinary medical care, all subject to the powers, rights, duties and responsibilities of the guardian of the minor and subject to any remaining parental rights and duties; an individual granted legal custody of a minor shall exercise his/her rights and responsibilities as custodian personally unless otherwise authorized by the Court entering the order.

"Minor" is a person under eighteen (18) years of age. "Minor-in-Need-of-Care" is a minor who is:

a. Neglected by a parent(s), guardian(s), Indian custodian(s), custodian(s) or other care-provider; or

b. Abused by a parent(s), guardian(s), Indian custodian(s), custodian(s) or other care-provider; or

c. A status offender.

"Neglect" occurs when a minor:

a. Has no parent(s), guardian(s), Indian custodian(s), custodian(s), or extended family member available and willing to care for him/her;

b. Has suffered, or is likely to certainly suffer a physical injury, inflicted upon him/her by other than accidental means, which causes or creates a substantial risk of death, disfigurement or impairment of bodily functions; or

c. Has not been provided with food, clothing, shelter, medical care, education or supervision adequate for his/her needs by his/her parent(s), guardian(s), Indian custodian(s) or custodian(s); or

d. Has committed delinquent acts as a result of parental pressure, guidance, or approval.

"Parent" includes a natural or adoptive parent but does not include persons whose parental rights have been terminated, nor does it include the unwed father whose paternity has not been acknowledged or established.

"Permanency Planning" is the procedure by which a permanent home is to be provided to every child, first, if possible, with the child(ren)'s parent(s) and, if that is not feasible within a reasonable time, with another person or family.

"Probable Cause" is reasonable cause or reasonable grounds to believe a fact, supported by sufficient evidence.

"Probation" is a legal status created by Court Order following an adjudication involving a violation of law by a minor, whereby the minor is permitted to remain in his/her home under prescribed conditions and under supervision of a probation officer, subject to return to the Court for further proceedings if any of the conditions prescribed are violated.

"Protective Supervision" is a legal status created by a Court Order in a proceeding not involving a delinquent act by a minor, whereby the minor is permitted to remain in his/her home under supervision and with assistance to correct the need for supervision or need of care which is provided by a social worker, probation officer or other agency designated by the Court.

"Restitution" is community service or financial reimbursement to the victim by the minor as ordered by the Court. It may include actual repairs done by the minor to the victim's damaged property. Financial reimbursement is limited to: actual damages for injury; loss of property; actual expenses for medical, psychiatric, and psychological treatment for injury to a person; and lost wages from physical injuries which are the direct result of the delinquent act.

"Shelter Care" is a home or living facility used as a temporary living place for a minor pending the return of the minor to his/her family or his/her placement in a residential facility designed for long-term placement.

"Status Offender" is a minor who:

a. Does not comply with the requirements of the compulsory school attendance Chapter of this Code; or

b. Disobeys, continually and habitually, the reasonable and lawful demands of his/her parent, guardian or custodian and is beyond parental control; or

c. Has left the home of his/her parent, guardian, or custodian without consent and has remained away for at least twenty-four (24) hours.

Chapter 10.02 Standards for the Protection and Benefit of Children

10.02.010 Best Interests of the Child.

All actions and decisions made under the authority of this Code shall be implemented to serve the best interests of the child. In determining the best interests of the child the following principles govern:

(a) A child's need for love, nurturing, protection and stability. A child must have a safe and nurturing home environment offering emotional support and comfort; the basic needs of food, clothing and shelter; reasonable medical care and protection from danger, violence, or exposure to harmful conduct including drug and alcohol abuse.

(b) A child's need for family. A child must have connection to loving and caring family members for guidance and nurturing. Although not all children have the benefit of family care, nothing can replace the primary role of loving parents and family in a child's life.

(c) A child's need for identity and development. A child must develop self-identity and awareness of his/her unique role within the larger community, including the child's cultural community. This is done by participation in cultural activities, speaking one's native language and having opportunities and encouragement to pursue education and enrichment.

(d) A child's need for happiness. A child cannot be happy unless his/her primary needs are met; but a child also needs opportunities for play and recreation, leisure time and other activities that the child enjoys, and possession of toys and other personal items of importance to the child.

10.02.020 Placement Priorities.

(a) Any placement under this Code shall be made for the best interests of the child(ren). All placement decisions should support the child(ren)'s affiliation with Pueblo of Picuris or Indian culture and provide the least restrictive environment which meets the child(ren)'s needs, and in the case of detention, assures the safety and protection of the public.

(b) With the understanding that the prioritization must be interpreted with flexibility and recognizing that the best interests of the child(ren) are primary, the following order of priority shall guide all placements:

(1) The child(ren) remain with the child(ren)'s family in the child(ren)'s home under conditions and restrictions established by the Court;

(2) Placement in the home of an extended family member residing on the Pueblo of Picuris;

(3) Placement in the home of an extended family member residing off the Pueblo of Picuris;

(4) Placement in a facility or home outside the Pueblo of Picuris which is licensed or certified by the Tribe, or licensed or certified for such placement by the State of New Mexico.

(c) The child(ren) shall be permitted to have appropriate involvement with family, tribal and cultural activities.

10.02.030 Shelter Standards.

(a) The Bureau of Indian Affairs Social Services Department, with the approval of the Tribal Council, shall adopt written regulations for the operation of a shelter care facility, foster care home, or detention facility licensed, certified, or approved by the Department. The regulations shall include standards for cleanliness, sanitation, heat, electricity, personnel, visiting privileges, occupancy limits, provisions for medical, dental and mental health care, and provisions for food, clothing and the possession of personal items.

(b) The Department shall enforce the following standards and develop regulations consistent with such standards, for the treatment of minors in a shelter care facility, foster care home, or a detention facility:

(1) A minor shall not be punished, ridiculed or criticized for expressing through speech, custom or dress in the minor's Indian or Tribal heritage;

(2) Subject to legitimate institutional regulations, a minor shall be allowed to wear his/her hair as he/she chooses;

(3) Incoming and outgoing mail may be inspected for contraband, but shall not be read;

(4) Whenever possible, the minor shall be allowed to attend the school in which he/she is enrolled. If the circumstances do not permit the minor to attend his/her regular school, arrangements shall be made to permit the minor to continue his/her education;

(5) A minor shall be allowed to attend family events and traditional ceremonies, provided that he/she is accompanied by a parent(s), guardian(s), Indian custodian(s), or custodian(s);

(6) A minor shall be given the opportunity to engage in physical exercise for at least one hour every day;

(7) A minor shall not be punished by physical force, solitary confinement, or deprivation of meals or family visits;

(8) A minor shall perform reasonable work duties such as maintenance of his/her bed and personal property, but shall not be required to perform work duties not commensurate with his/her age, physical, mental, or emotional abilities.

(c) In addition to and notwithstanding the provisions of Paragraph B, the following standards shall apply to any placement of a minor in a detention facility:

(1) A minor shall wear only the clothing supplied by the detention facility;

(2) If the minor is unable to attend a local school, schoolwork and education assistance at the minor's level of development shall be provided for the minor;

(3) With prior Court Order, a minor shall be allowed to attend the funeral and related activities of family or extended family members, or to attend traditional ceremonies, provided that he/she is accompanied by the parent(s), guardian(s), Indian custodian(s), or custodian(s), and returns immediately to the detention facility at the conclusion of the period set by the Court.

(4) If reasonable belief exists that a minor may cause physical injury to him/herself or others, he/she shall be placed in a supervised holding cell and monitored on a prescribed schedule and referred for immediate treatment by medical and/or psychological personnel.

10.02.040 Duty to Report Abuse and Neglect.

(a) Persons who have a reasonable cause to suspect a minor has been abused or neglected shall report the abuse or neglect to the Bureau of Indian Affairs Social Services Department and to the Bureau of Indian Affairs Law Enforcement Office.

(b) Those persons who are required to report suspected abuse or neglect are any physician, nurse, other medical, mental health professional, principal, teacher, other school official, social worker, child care worker, other child care staff, including foster parents, residential care or institutional personnel, counselor, peace officer, judge, attorney, other judicial officer and any other person required to report pursuant to Federal or Tribal law. This paragraph shall not be interpreted to override ethical rules defining the attorney-client privilege.

(c) Any person may make a report of suspected abuse or neglect.

(d) Those persons reporting, except those specified in Paragraph B, may remain anonymous if the Court so determines.

(e) All persons or agencies reporting abuse or neglect, in good faith, shall be immune from civil or criminal liability.

10.02.050 Records Maintenance and Protection.

(a) A record of all hearings under this Code shall be made and preserved until the jurisdiction of the Court has ended. All Court and law enforcement records concerning a minor shall be kept separate from the records of adults.

(b) All records are confidential and shall not be open to inspection other than by Court personnel. The following persons are subject to regulations adopted by the Court:

(1) The minor and his/her attorney;

(2) The minor's parent(s), guardian(s), Indian custodian(s), or custodian(s);

(3) The Juvenile Probation Officer or child welfare caseworker;

(4) The prosecutor; and

(5) Any other person granted permission by Court Order.

(c) Each person who inspects a minor's record shall, in writing agree, to maintain the confidentiality of the record. Failure to abide by the agreement shall constitute Contempt of Court.

(d) All records of a minor shall be sealed when the minor is no longer subject to the Court's jurisdiction, and thereafter, released only by Court Order following notice to the parties, and, only if needed, for a Court proceeding concerning a serious criminal charge or similar circumstance of compelling need. This paragraph shall not be construed to permit the release of information for other purposes, such as background checks conducted for employment, licensing, or security clearances. Adoption records shall be released as allowed in Section 10- 13-9.

Chapter 10.05 Jurisdiction

10.05.010 Jurisdiction of the Pueblo of Picuris Children's Court.

The Children's Court of the Pueblo of Picuris has exclusive original jurisdiction of the following:

(a) Juvenile offenders except in those cases transferred to Tribal Court pursuant to this Code;

(b) Minors-in-need-of-care;

(c) Termination of parental rights;

(d) Adoption of minors;

(e) Award of custody or appointment of custodian or guardian of minors;

(f) Commitment of minors;

(g) Emancipation;

(h) Minors before a State court who are residents or domiciliary of the Tribe as required by the Indian Child Welfare Act, 25 U.S.C. Section 1911 (a);

(i) Minors before a State court who are not residents or domiciliary of the Tribe and transfer of jurisdiction has been requested and accepted as required by the Indian Child Welfare Act, 25 U.S.C. Section 1911 (b); or

(j) Adults as set out in this Code.

The Children's Court of the Pueblo of Picuris has concurrent jurisdiction over any minor who commits an act, which is deemed illegal by federal, state, municipal or other tribe's law, while within any of those jurisdictions when that minor is an enrolled member of the Pueblo of Picuris.

10.05.030 Transfer to Tribal Court of Alleged Juvenile Offender.

(a) The prosecutor may file a petition requesting the Court to transfer the minor to the jurisdiction of the Tribal Court if the minor is at least sixteen (16) years of age and is alleged to have committed an act that would be considered a crime if committed by an adult.

(b) The Court shall conduct a hearing within ten (10) days of filing of the petition to determine whether jurisdiction of the minor should be transferred to Tribal Court.

(c) The Juvenile Probation Officer shall prepare and present a written report to the Court at least three (3) days before the transfer hearing. The report shall contain:

(1) Information on the nature and seriousness of the alleged offense(s);

(2) The nature and condition of the minor as evidenced by his/her age as well as mental and physical condition; and

(3) Past record of offenses and Court efforts at rehabilitation

(d) The prosecutor and other attorneys representing parties may also file with the Court written recommendations within the same time limit. All reports shall be served on all parties at the same time they are filed.

(e) The following factors shall be considered by the Court when determining whether to transfer jurisdiction of the minor to Tribal Court:

(1) The nature and seriousness of the offense(s) with which the minor is charged;

(2) The nature and condition of the minor, as evidences by his/her age as well as mental and physical condition; and

(3) The past record of offenses and Court efforts at rehabilitation.

(f) The Court may transfer jurisdiction of the minor to Tribal Court if the Court finds clear and convincing evidence that there are no reasonable prospects for rehabilitating the minor through resources available to the Court and either of the following exist:

(1) The past offenses allegedly committed by the minor indicate a pattern of conduct which constitutes a substantial danger to the public; or

(2) The offense with which the minor is charged as a juvenile offender indicates conduct which constitutes substantial danger to the public.

(g) The Court's Order under this section is a final Order for the purposes of appeal.

10.05.050 Adults.

(a) Parental Responsibility.

(1) Parent as Party: The parent of the child may be made a party to a petition if the child is alleged to be a minor-in-need-of-care or a juvenile offender.

(2) Dispositions: The Court may order the parent to submit to counseling, participate in any probation or other treatment program ordered by the Court and, if the child is committed for institutionalization, to participate in any institutional treatment or counseling program including attendance at the site of the institution.

(3) Fines: The parent is not liable for a fine his/her child may be ordered to pay.

(4) Cost of Support: The Court shall order the parent to pay the reasonable cost or part of the cost of support and treatment of the child if:

(A) The child is adjudicated a minor-in-need-of-care, or a juvenile offender; and

(B) The Court orders this child placed with an agency, institution or an individual other than the parent.

(b) Other Adults: The Court shall join as a party any adult necessary for proper disposition of any case heard pursuant to this Code.

(c) Enforcement: The Court may enforce any orders issued under this section by use of its contempt power.

10.09.060 Preliminary Inquiry.

(a) The purpose of the preliminary inquiry is to determine the best interest of the minor with regard to any action to be taken. In determining the best interest of the minor, the Court shall examine whether probable cause exists to believe the alleged act was committed and whether continued detention or shelter care is necessary pending further proceedings.

(b) Time Limits.

(1) Minor in Custody: The preliminary inquiry shall be held within seventy-two (72) hours of the beginning of the time of detention or shelter care.

(2) Minor Released from Custody: The preliminary inquiry shall be held within three (3) days of release of the minor from detention or shelter care.

(3) Minor Never in Custody: The preliminary inquiry shall be held within ten (10) days of the filing of a complaint.

(c) Presence of Minor's Parents or Guardian: If the minor's parent(s) or guardian is not present at the preliminary inquiry, the Court shall determine what efforts have been made to notify and to obtain the presence of the parent(s) or guardian. If it appears that further efforts are likely to produce the parent(s) or guardian, the Court shall recess for not more than twenty-four (24) hours and direct the Bureau of Indian Affairs Law Enforcement or Pueblo of Picuris Tribal Sheriff to make continued efforts to obtain the presence of a parent, guardian, or custodian.

(d) Dismissal of Complaint and Release of a Minor: If the Court determines that there is no probable cause to believe that the minor has committed the alleged act, the complaint shall be dismissed without prejudice and the minor is to be released.

(e) Release from Custody: If the Court determines that there is probable cause to believe that the minor has committed the alleged act and that the minor is not in need of detention or shelter care, the minor shall be released to the custody of his/her parent(s) or guardian pending final disposition of the matter. Otherwise, the minor shall continue to be held in custody until further order of the Court.

(f) Criteria for Detention or Shelter Care: The Court may order detention or shelter care, or order it to continue, if the Court finds probable cause exists to believe the minor committed the alleged act and:

(1) The act is serious enough to warrant continued detention or shelter care; or

(2) There is reasonable cause to believe the minor will run away so that he/she will be unavailable for further proceedings; or

(3) There is reasonable cause to believe that the minor will commit a serious act causing damage to person or property.

(g) Plea and Disposition: A minor may admit or deny that he/she committed the alleged illegal act at the preliminary inquiry. If the minor denies committing the act, the Court shall proceed as directed in this Code. If the minor wishes to admit he/she committed the alleged act, the Court must first determine that:

(1) The minor has a full understanding of his/her rights under this Code; and

(2) The minor has full understanding of the consequences of admitting that he/she committed the alleged act; and

(3) The minor has not stated any facts that would be a defense. If the Court determines that the minor does not understand his/her rights or the consequences of admitting to have committed the alleged act or the minor has stated a defense, the Court shall order either an informal adjustment conference be held or that a petition be filed. If the Court determines that the minor understands his/her rights and the consequences of admitting to having committed the alleged act and has not stated a defense, the Court may proceed to the dispositional hearing as allowed by this Code.

Chapter 10.12 Termination of Parental Rights

10.12.010 Purpose.

The purpose of this chapter is to provide for voluntary or non-voluntary termination of the parent-child relationship by Court Order. Non-voluntary termination of parental rights over a child is a serious matter and an action that the Court may take only after all remedies have been exhausted in the attempt to maintain the stability of the family. Further, it is the purpose of this chapter to provide meaningful and clear standards to be applied to cases involving termination of parental rights and to ensure competent, stable and on-going care of the child by prompt and final adjudication.

10.12.030 Grounds for Non-Voluntary Termination.

(a) Termination Allowed: The Court may terminate a parent's rights when the Court finds beyond a reasonable doubt that the parent is: (1) unfit; (2) that the conduct or condition of the parent is such as to render him/her unable to care for the child and that such conduct or condition is unlikely to change within one year.

(b) Factors to be considered: In determining unfitness, conduct, or condition, the Court shall consider, but is not limited to, any of the following:

(1) The parent has abandoned the minor; or

(2) Emotional illness, mental illness or mental deficiency of the parent of such duration or nature as to render the parent unable to care for the ongoing physical, mental and emotional needs of the child within one (1) year from the date of determination of illness; or

(3) Abuse or neglect of the minor as defined by this Code; or

(4) Excessive use of intoxicating liquors or illegal substances or drugs over a period of one or more years; or

(5) Imprisonment of a single parent for a period of one or more years; or

(6) Adjudication by a court or a plea of guilty by a parent to the charge that the parent, either intentionally or recklessly, willfully or wantonly caused the death or injury of a minor's sibling; or

(7) Failure of the parent to provide a home or reasonable substitute physical care and maintenance where custody is lodged with others; or

(8) Failure of the parent to maintain regular visitation or other contact with the child as designated in a plan to reunite the child with the parent; or

(9) Failure of the parent to maintain consistent contact or communication with the child over a period of one year; or

(10) Rehabilitation of the parent has been unsuccessful.

(c) Parent Unknown: The rights of the parent may be terminated, as provided herein, if the Court finds that the child has been left under such circumstances that the identity of the parent is unknown and cannot be ascertained, despite diligent searching, and the parent has not come forward to claim the child within three (3) months following the finding of the child.

(d) Best Interests of the Child: In considering terminating the rights of a parent, the Court shall give primary consideration to the best interests of the child as shown by his/her physical, mental, and emotional condition and needs.
